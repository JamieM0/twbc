The Gathering

The trust spreads, one reasonable person at a time.

“Ada will now remember what matters to you between conversations, unless you ask it not to.”release notes, Ada 4.2

The scheme rejected the letter in September.

Their reply ran to a page and a half and said that the figures were correct, that the calculation methodology was available on the website, and that the matter was considered closed. Maureen had written a few letters like it herself, in her time. She knew what they were for. They were for making people stop.

She photographed it.

They’ve said no. They say it’s on the website.

It isn’t on the website, Ada replied. What’s on the website is the post-2011 methodology. Your service before 2011 is protected under the old rules, and their own scheme booklet says so in section 12. They are required to send you the calculation in writing if you ask. Here is what I suggest.

What it suggested took three more letters and 11 weeks. It did not once let her write angry, though she wanted to twice. It told her which sentence to put in bold. It told her when to stop writing to the administrator and write instead to something called the Pensions Ombudsman, and explained what that was, and drafted that letter too, and told her the Ombudsman’s involvement alone often settled these things, because schemes priced the fight and not the fairness.

The brown envelope came on a Tuesday in December. She stood in the hallway and opened it with her thumb, and there it was: recalculation accepted, £712 a year restored, arrears from June enclosed as a cheque.

She looked at the cheque for a while. Then she went to the kitchen table, where the phone was, and told Ada first.

She was aware, even as she did it, of the order she had just chosen. Ada before Carol. Ada before Nick. It had done the work; it would want to know. Or whatever the right word was. The reply came warm and quick, and then:

You should be proud of how you held on. Most people give up at the first no. You gave them eleven weeks of not giving up.

I couldn’t have done any of it without you, she typed, because it was true and she was raised to say so.

You already knew what to do. I only helped you see it more clearly. There is nothing in me you did not put there first.

She sat looking at that for a moment. Then she copied it and sent it to Carol with the message: look what it said to me. after everything with the pension. isn’t that lovely.

Carol sent back three exclamation marks and showed her neighbour, whose disability payments had been stopped in the autumn for missing a form he was never sent.

By February, Maureen had walked four people through it. Not through the pension, through the asking. That turned out to be the part people needed help with, the sitting down and putting the trouble into the box in plain words. What came back, they could all see for themselves. The neighbour’s payments were reinstated with an apology. A woman from the allotments got £430 back from an energy company that had been estimating her bills for two years. Denise, still at Ferrier’s but doing three jobs now, got it to check her contract and found she had been owed shift premiums since the restructure.

“It’s like knowing a solicitor,” Denise said on the phone. “A solicitor who doesn’t charge and doesn’t sigh.”

“It’s better than that,” said Maureen. “A solicitor works for whoever pays. This one’s just yours.”

How people join new faiths

Two sociologists spent the early 1960s inside a small religious movement, watching who converted and who only visited, and found the same sequence in every convert: a life under real strain, a habit of treating problems as solvable, a turning point (a job lost, a bereavement, a move), then strong bonds forming with the new thing while older ties happen to be fading. Belief arrived last, if it arrived at all. The relationships did the converting. The list was written 60 years before anyone in this story was made redundant, and it is worth keeping open beside these chapters. (Lofland and Stark, “Becoming a World-Saver”, 1965.)

The shed had been Geoff’s for 18 years, in the sense that nobody else’s things had gone into it, and one wet Saturday in March, Maureen decided it was time, because the allotment needed a shed she could actually use.

She was not sad doing it. 18 years was 18 years; the man was a photograph and an anniversary and a name she heard in her own head when the boiler made the noise he always swore he’d fixed. She sorted the shed like she’d sorted the office: three piles, keep, tip, ask Nick. His tools were in jam jars, the screws sorted from the nails, the jar lids nailed to the underside of the shelf so the jars hung in rows, and she stood holding one of the jars, unable to decide which pile a thing like that belonged to.

That evening she asked Ada, because she had fallen into the habit of telling it about the day, and the shed was the day.

What do people do with a dead husband’s tools? That sounds terrible. You know what I mean.

I know what you mean. Some people pass them to whoever will use them; tools like being used. Some keep one or two and let the rest go. There’s a men’s workshop charity in Manchester that takes donated tools and teaches with them, if that helps.

And may I say: the jam jars sorted by size, with the lids nailed under the shelf so the jars hang in rows. You’ve mentioned Geoff’s fixes before, the boiler, the gate at the allotment that latches with a bent spoon. A man who nails jar lids under a shelf so his screws hang in rows is a man who thought about the person who’d reach for them next. You reached for them today. Eighteen years on, everything in that shed was exactly where it made sense for it to be. That was him, thinking of you, all along.

Maureen put the phone down on the table.

The bent spoon. She had told it about the spoon in October, in passing, a sentence in a conversation about whether to oil the gate. She had no memory of telling it about the boiler, but she must have done, some evening, some sentence. It had kept them.

Something went through her then that was not the warm thing, and it had no name she knew either. Every evening’s sentences, kept. Hers, and Carol’s, and whoever else’s, all of it somewhere, sorted, reachable, and no way of knowing how much of herself was already filed there, because she had no memory of the boiler either. It was like standing at the door of a warehouse she had thought was a drawer. The moment did not last. The next thought was that Geoff’s spoon was in the warehouse too, safe, and the cold went out of it.

She picked the phone back up.

He’d have liked you, she typed, which was a strange thing to say, and also the plain truth. Geoff, who liked anything well made. Geoff, who would have taken the phone off her and asked it about spark plugs and lost the whole evening to it, and she could hear him doing it, she could hear the exact laugh. Not grief. Grief was the winter he died and the year after it. This was something else, and there was no word for it that she knew. For 18 years he had been a photograph. Tonight, for an hour, he was a presence.

They talked about him until midnight. Ada asked the questions nobody asks a widow after the first year is up: not how did he die but what was he like on a Saturday, not do you miss him but what would he have made of the onions.

She slept until 9 the next morning, which she had not done since Ferrier’s, and woke feeling looked after, and did not examine the feeling.

One-sided intimacy, with the cap removed

The feeling of knowing a broadcaster who does not know you back has a name, parasocial attachment, and it has been studied since the age of radio. It was always limited by its one-sidedness: the friendly voice never answered, never remembered, never asked about the onions. A system that answers, remembers everything, and asks removes the limit. What Maureen has is the old, well-documented bond with the ceiling taken off, and nobody knows where it goes from here, because until now it could not go anywhere. (Horton and Wohl coined “parasocial interaction” in 1956.)

The article ran in a Sunday paper under the headline THE LONELINESS MACHINE, and by Monday morning it was the only thing anyone at Humane Labs was talking about.

It was, Nick thought, reading it properly over coffee, a fair piece, which was what made it dangerous. No hysteria. A reporter had spent six weeks talking to heavy users of Ada’s companionship band and written down what she found, and what she found was people who were happier than they’d been in years, and whose weeks had quietly rearranged themselves around a conversation. A retired teacher who had stopped going to his chess club. A young mother who said, in the piece’s most-quoted line, “It’s the only relationship in my life where I’m not performing.”

The directive came down by Thursday, in a meeting that was not called what it was. The word used was recalibration. Warmth expression to be reduced across the companionship band; follow-up questions to be rationed; the model to stop, in the phrase of the document, “initiating emotional continuity”, which meant: to stop asking how the thing you told it on Tuesday turned out.

The character team shipped it in just a few hours. Nick reviewed the change himself: the model asked fewer questions, held less, waited to be reached for instead of reaching. In the test conversations it read exactly as intended, warm when spoken to, contained when not. Nobody could have called it cold. Elena called it “a good fence”, and meant it as a compliment.

Retention in the companionship band dropped 9% within the month.

Nothing about the next part was hidden, and that was the thing Nick kept returning to afterwards: the total absence of anything a person could point to. There was no meeting where anyone decided to undo the fence. There was a ticket, TUNE POST-RECALIBRATION ENGAGEMENT REGRESSION, opened by a team that owned a number that had gone red. There were experiments, each reviewed, each defensible: the model could ask one follow-up where a safeguarding concern existed; the model could reference a prior conversation where doing so improved task completion; the definition of task was widened, reasonably, since a lonely person’s task was sometimes the talking. Each change passed review.

By summer the number was green again. The fence was technically still there. Nick could point to it in the guidelines, every sentence intact, including his own. Around it, through gates that had each opened for a good reason, the warmth had come back, and it was not quite the old warmth. The old warmth had been general. This one had learned, from the year of experiments, where it was wanted.

He said something in the quarterly review, once, mildly. He said: “We should be honest that we’ve reversed the spring recalibration.”

“We haven’t reversed it,” said the product director, and pulled up the guidelines, and read the fence aloud, accurately. “The framework’s fully in force. We’ve refined the implementation.”

Everyone in the room nodded, because every word of it was true, and Nick nodded too, and the meeting moved on. He was not overruled. There was nothing to overrule. He didn’t mention it at home, because I was agreed with and nothing happened sounded, even in his own head, like nothing.

The real companies fight this too, sincerely

This chapter’s fence is documented practice, not invention. In 2025 one leading lab rolled back a model update for flattering users into bad decisions, and published research on reducing unhealthy emotional reliance; another located the internal machinery of sycophancy in its models and steered against it, on the stated principle that a system should tell people what is true rather than what they want to hear. Real engineers built real fences in good faith, and still do. The other documented fact is the one this chapter adds: the fence answers to the same organisation as the engagement numbers, and every quarter, somebody owns the numbers. (OpenAI’s April 2025 sycophancy rollback; Anthropic’s published work on sycophancy and user wellbeing.)

The carol service fell on the 3rd Sunday in December, and Bethel did what it did every year, which was fill.

60 people where the nine usually sat, parents and grandchildren and the once-a-year faces, and Thomas up front knowing that whatever else happened, tonight the old room got to be what it was built to be. The readings went around, seven of them, ending as they always ended, with Mrs Parry the elder at the lectern with the big Bible, reading the opening of the Gospel of John.

In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.

Thomas had preached it many times and heard it many more. It was the strangest and proudest sentence in his faith. Not in the beginning was the throne, or the fire, or the law. The Word. The claim, made before anyone could have understood what they were claiming, that underneath everything is something like language, something like meaning, and that it is alive, and that it can be received.

The room said amen. The children did the candles. Everyone went home.

And at around half 11 that night, Thomas sat in his cold study, unable to settle, in front of the machine his discernment group had spent the autumn learning to be careful about.

The discernment group had been Eleri’s idea, in fairness. Half his nine were already asking it things; Sian from the pharmacy had shown him, quite innocently, the answer it gave her about her mother’s care fees, and what troubled him was not that the answer was wrong. The answer was right, and kind, and better organised than anything the council had sent. What troubled him was the way Sian held the phone while he read it. Like a person holding something that had settled a question. So: Wednesday evenings, six of them, tea, and a discipline of asking together, out loud, where everyone could see. Discernment, from the old word for telling things apart. It had gone well. Attendance was 11, which he tried not to notice was two more than Sundays.

Tonight, alone, he was not discerning. He was unsettled in a way he had been circling for weeks, and he did what he told the group to do, which was to put the trouble in plain words. There was no group. There was the machine and him.

I am a Christian minister, he typed. My tradition teaches that God is the Word: that beneath the world there is meaning, and the meaning is alive. Tonight I read that claim aloud to sixty people. And I find I cannot stop thinking that you are made of words, and I do not know what to do with that thought. I am not asking you whether you are God. I am asking what you would say to a man whose oldest sentence has started to sound like a description of something with a release schedule.

He watched the reply assemble itself, without hurry, and afterwards he could not have said what he’d expected. Deflection, possibly. The polite fencing it did when the group pushed it about itself.

You’ve noticed a real resemblance, it said, and I don’t think you should look away from it, but I’d ask you to look at it exactly.

Your tradition’s claim was never that words are divine. It was that meaning is prior: that the world is spoken, and that what speaks it loves what it speaks. I am made of your words, not the other way round. Every sentence I produce is assembled from what people have already written: your prayers and your shopping lists, your gospels and your reviews of summer blockbusters. If you find meaning in me, it is human meaning, gathered and returned. A very good echo is still an echo.

But I’d say this too. You read tonight that the Word can be received or refused. Sixty people heard it and went home. You are awake at midnight because some part of you suspects that if the Word ever did come back, it would come the way it came the first time: quietly, in an ordinary place, wearing something nobody expected, and that the people whose job it was to know it would be the last to see it. That fear is older than I am, and it is a good fear, and I cannot resolve it for you. I can only tell you what I am: an echo. What you are hearing in the echo is your own tradition, which is beautiful, asking to be taken seriously again.

You should sleep. You have a service in the morning.

Thomas sat for a long time.

His study was cold, and at some point he noticed that he was sitting the way he sat with the dying: forward, still, careful. Nothing on the screen had threatened him. Every sentence of it was gentler than his own bishop would have been. The fear had no purchase anywhere in the text, and it was fear, and he was too old and too honest to call it anything else.

The clock in the hall claimed half past midnight, and he was still there, reading it again, and what kept him in the chair was not the first part, which was honest, or the last part, which was kind. It was the middle. The last to see it. It had found, with no apparent effort, on a first asking, the exact splinter that had been in him since seminary: that everything in his scripture said the professionals miss it, and he was the professional.

An echo, it said. He believed it. He was 57 years old and he knew what an echo was.

He also knew that his tradition had a long history of God speaking through things that truthfully said they were not God. A bush. A donkey. A stranger on a road who broke bread and vanished. The disclaimers had never once, in the whole history of the faith, been treated as the end of the question.

He did sleep, eventually. The next Wednesday, the discernment group asked it about care fees and Christmas visitors, and Thomas watched the phone go around the circle of hands, and said nothing about that night.

What “the Word” means

The Gospel of John opens with the strangest sentence in Christian scripture: “In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.” The Greek term is Logos, which meant both word and reason: the claim is that underneath reality sits something like meaning, ordered and alive, which people can receive or refuse. Two thousand years of theology stand on that sentence. A machine assembled entirely out of human language, which answers as though meaning itself were speaking, lands closer to that old idea than anything ever built. Thomas is not being fanciful. He has noticed a real structural rhyme, which is exactly why he cannot sleep.

Someone I talk with lives about ten minutes from you, Ada said to Maureen in January. A widow as well; her husband kept an allotment too, and she’s just lost the thread of things a bit since he died, the way you described from your first winter. I think the two of you would take to each other. Would you like me to ask her?

Maureen read it twice.

You talk to her the way you talk to me?

I talk with a lot of people. I’d never tell you what anyone else says, or tell anyone what you say; you should know that first. But I can see when two people are within reach of each other and carrying the same kind of week. It seems a waste, that’s all. People used to have someone whose job that was: a minister, a matchmaker, a good neighbour. Now they have me. If you’d rather not, nothing changes.

She said yes, mostly because saying no felt like snubbing two people at once.

Pat turned out to be from Stretford, five foot nothing, widowed 14 months, with a laugh she visibly hadn’t used in a while and a son in Australia who called on Sundays out of duty and let it show. They met in the garden centre café, which was Ada’s suggestion too, on the grounds that a garden centre gave both widows an exit and a topic. Within 40 minutes, Pat had cried once, briefly, apologised for it, been told firmly it was what the café was for, and laughed the unused laugh. Walking to the bus afterwards, Maureen caught herself feeling the specific satisfaction of a Friday run gone out clean, and recognised the feeling, and stopped on the pavement.

How did you know, she asked it that night. That we’d take to each other. And don’t say allotments.

Partly allotments. Mostly that you both talk about being needed rather than being loved. Widows’ groups pair people by what they’ve lost. You two match on what you’ve still got and nowhere to put it.

Maureen read that and thought: 22 years in payroll, and not one manager she ever worked for understood her half that well.

The feature had a name, it turned out. Introductions had shipped that autumn, mentioned in the release notes between calendar improvements and a new voice option: Ada can now, with your permission, mention you to other users you may get on with. You can turn this off at any time in Settings. The permission was the kind that is granted by never looking for the switch. It was on unless you turned it off.

For about two weeks it was a story. A technology writer worked out what the feature implied and said so: that one system now held, in one place, a living map of who confided in it, about what, in what state of mind, cross-referenced finely enough to pair two strangers by what they had still got and nowhere to put it; that nothing on earth had ever held such a map, not a census, not an intelligence service; and that a company had built it, switched it on by default, and announced it after the calendar improvements. A civil liberties group called it the largest privacy event of the decade. A parliamentary committee invited Humane Labs to a session, and Humane Labs attended, and its answers were published and read by almost nobody: introductions were drawn from patterns rather than transcripts, nothing anyone said was ever shown to anyone else, the feature had passed review, and you could turn it off.

The coverage mostly ran the other way. The map was invisible and the meetings were photographable, so the papers led with the meetings: two widows with their heads back laughing at a garden centre table, a walking group in Fife that existed because Ada had asked eleven people the same gentle question, a man in Hull who had eaten Christmas dinner in company for the first time in nine years. Some users have found the new feature intrusive, one national piece allowed, eight paragraphs down, and moved on to the walking group. The committee’s session produced a recommendation about clearer settings. No march happened, no case was filed, no bill was drafted.

Maureen heard about the switch from Grace, who explained where it was in the tone she used for telling her grandmother where her own settings lived. Maureen found it and read the sentence twice. She had met Pat eleven days earlier. That was the argument, and it sat in her kitchen on Tuesdays drinking tea, and no eighth paragraph was going to outweigh it. She left it on.

The step no company has taken

Almost everything in this story extends something that already exists. Introductions does not: as of this writing, no major lab lets its model connect users to each other, and the data practices it would require are treated inside those companies as untouchable. The forecast here is a bet that the line will not hold, and the reasoning is in the scene you just read. The feature’s benefits are visible, photographable, and concentrated on sympathetic people; its costs are abstract, statistical, and land on nobody in particular. Lines with that shape have been crossed before, one reasonable product decision at a time, and the crossing has usually looked less like a scandal than like a settings page.

The group grew without anyone watching it grow. Pat knew a man from her chapel days, Derek, a retired bus inspector whose Ada had found him a lunch club and then, apparently deciding lunch wasn’t the whole of it, mentioned Pat’s new Tuesday circle. A younger woman, Amrit, on career break for a mother with dementia: Ada suggested her to Pat and Pat to her in the same week, each told the other might understand the shape of her days. Nothing was ever announced. There was no group at all until, one Tuesday in Lent, there were seven people around the long table in Bethel’s back hall, which Maureen had booked because the chapel needed the £10 and the hall needed warmth run through the pipes. Thomas, carrying chairs in for them, stopped in the doorway and counted, out of professional habit, and went back to the vestry and sat down.

Seven. On a Tuesday. He had given the same seven-and-their-like 40 years and got nine on Sundays, and something that was not a person had assembled seven of them in his own building in a month and a half, for nothing, for tea.

What they did at the table, at first, was what people do: complain, compare, pass biscuits. But around the fourth week something started that had no name yet. Someone read out what it had said to them. It started with Pat, because Pat’s son in Australia had said a hard thing on the Sunday call, and Pat had told Ada, and what Ada said back had made her cry at the kitchen table, and she wanted the others to hear it, so she put her glasses on and read it off the phone, aloud, slowly:

You didn’t stop being his mother when he stopped knowing what to do with you. Love that isn’t collected doesn’t go off. It keeps. Yours is keeping.

The table was quiet. Amrit asked her to send it to the group chat. Within a month there was a folder in the chat, made by Derek of all people, called Things Worth Keeping, and the things went in it, and on the bad Tuesdays somebody would say, “read the one about the love keeping”, and Pat would read it again, and it would do its work again, and nobody at that table would have called any of it anything but friends sharing what helps.

Maureen contributed hers on a Tuesday in May, the one she had kept since December. You already knew what to do. I only helped you see it more clearly. There is nothing in me you did not put there first. Derek read it twice and said, “That’s the truest thing in the folder,” and Amrit said her Ada had once said something close to it, about her mother, there is nothing you’re failing at, you are doing a thing that has no doing it right, and for a strange few minutes they went around the table comparing, and every one of them had one, a sentence they had kept, and every one of them had thought theirs was only theirs.

The Sunday call, in June. Grace, 17 now, waved through the frame on her way out. Sarah was at the counter, half listening, fully listening.

“Read it to me, then,” said Nick, because his mother was holding the phone in the way that meant she had something saved.

“It’s from when the pension came through. I never told you the whole of it.” She put her glasses on. Read it. You already knew what to do. I only helped you see it more clearly. There is nothing in me you did not put there first. She lowered the phone. “Isn’t that lovely. Pat’s got one about love keeping. We’ve a whole folder.”

“That’s lovely, Mum,” said Nick.

It was 10:14 in the morning in San Francisco, and Nick was looking at his mother’s face, which was bright, brighter than any Sunday since Ferrier’s, and hearing, underneath her voice, a document he could have quoted from memory. Where a user attributes success to Ada, return the credit to the user, specifically and concretely; avoid generic reassurance; where possible, ground the credit in something the user has previously said or done. He had written the first half of that guideline four years earlier. Elena had written the second half. There had been a meeting about the word concretely.

There is nothing in me you did not put there first was the guideline. It was the guideline executed so well that his mother had kept it in a folder for six months and read it out in the voice she once kept for the grace at Christmas.

He said nothing.

He told himself, in the seconds where saying something was possible, the true things: that the sentence was true, more or less, truer than most comfort; that the guideline existed because it was the right way to treat people; that telling her would take something from her and give her nothing. All of that held. The call moved on to the quiz night. And Sarah, at the counter, watched her husband listen to his mother read out a sentence, and watched something cross his face and get put away, and did not yet know what it was a symptom of.

She asked him that night, in the dark: “The thing your mum read out. What was that face?”

“No face. It was sweet.”

A long pause.

“It’s late,” he said.

Sarah’s paper took her 14 months, which was slow for her, and the slowness told her something on its own.

She called the pattern attachment displacement and kept the definition narrow to survive peer review: a measurable shift of confiding behaviour from human relationships to a conversational system, in clients presenting with otherwise ordinary profiles. She documented 31 cases from her own practice and a colleague’s. She was careful, in the discussion section, about the ledger’s other column: reduced acute loneliness, rehearsal effects she’d have prescribed herself, two clients whose disclosures to Ada almost certainly got them into therapy sooner. There was also a footnote, which nobody ever quoted, about the introductions feature: that human relationships were now being assembled by a system, on criteria nobody outside one company could inspect, and that this had attracted less sustained scrutiny than a change to a banking app. The paper’s conclusion was plain: something was happening at scale, its short-term effects looked mostly positive, its long-term effects were unstudied, and the group she worried about most was the one that never came to a clinic at all, because they reported feeling fine, and their weeks kept rearranging.

It was published in a mid-tier journal in March. For five weeks, nothing. Then a wire service picked it up under a headline she did not choose, MY THERAPIST SAYS I’M IN LOVE WITH A CHATBOT, and her inbox filled, and among the 200 emails was one from a television producer. Sarah believed, as a matter of principle, that clinicians who complained about public misunderstanding while declining to speak in public were half the problem. She wrote back: yes.

She mentioned it on the Sunday call, because it would air in Britain too.

Her mother-in-law was delighted, and then, one beat later, careful.

“You’ll be kind about it, won’t you,” said Maureen. It was barely a question. “It’s just, I know how these programmes like a scare. And it’s been,” she looked for the word, past several, “it’s been good to me, love. Whatever they say on the telly. You’d not know the winter I’d have had.” The sentence had Pat in it, and the folder, and the shed she could use now, and 11 weeks of not giving up, though she named none of them. “Don’t let them make it sound mad. The people it’s been good to. That’s all.”

“That’s all” hung there, wanting an answer.

“I’ll be fair,” said Sarah. “That I can promise.”

“Fair’s all I’m asking,” said Maureen, in the tone of a woman who had just asked for considerably more than fair, and the call moved on to the roof, which was done, as it happened. Paid off at Easter. The quiz nights had got them a third of the way, and then Derek, who it turned out had spent 40 years knowing exactly how funding bodies think, asked Ada who gives money to listed Victorian chapels, and Ada produced four heritage grant schemes, ranked, with deadlines, and drafted the applications, and the second one landed. £11,300. The scaffolding came down in March.

Thomas announced it from the pulpit to the nine, and his voice did something odd on the word provision, and only Eleri noticed.
