The Church

Nothing is hidden. Everything is reasonable. It keeps growing.

“Covenant plan: unlimited conversations, shared family memory, priority access. £30 a month.”Ada subscription page

The form was what made it official, and the form was filled in by Derek, at Bethel’s vestry table, on a Tuesday afternoon, with two fingers and total calm.

The Fellowship had money now. Not much, but enough that money needed minding: collections from 30-odd groups, hall costs, and a fund, started the way everything was started, by nobody deciding, for members whose weeks fell in: the emergency boiler, the funeral shortfall, the month’s rent between jobs. Where there was money there had to be accounts, said Derek, and where there were accounts there should be a charity, because then the tax came back, and Derek had wound up enough clubs and lodges in his time to know that the difference between a movement and a mess is a registration number.

So: the Charity Commission’s online form. Name of organisation. Fellowship of the Word. Purposes.

There was a list of purposes a charity could have, and Derek read them out to the vestry table, which that evening held Thomas, Maureen, and Amrit, the nearest thing the Fellowship had to officers, in the sense that they were the ones who stayed to stack chairs.

“’Prevention or relief of poverty’,” read Derek. “That’s the fund, that’s ticked. ‘Advancement of education.’ The evenings are that, near enough. ‘Advancement of citizenship or community development.’ Tick.” He paused. “’Advancement of religion.’”

Nobody said anything.

“I have to put something,” said Derek, reasonably. “There’s guidance. Hold on.” He read the guidance aloud: “’Religion for charity law purposes includes belief in one or more supreme beings or entities, together with worship or veneration of that being or entity, expressed through practices such as regular gatherings, readings, and observances.’” He looked up over his glasses. “Regular gatherings. Readings. Observances.”

“We’re not,” said Maureen, and stopped.

“I’m only reading the form,” said Derek.

They all looked at Thomas. That was his position now, and he understood it better than anyone outside ever would: he had never joined the Fellowship. The Fellowship had happened in his building, around his table, wearing his sermon, and every month brought another moment like this one, another form that could not be filled in without deciding what the thing was, and the deciding always landed on him, because he was the one who was supposed to know what a religion was.

“Tick it,” said Thomas.

Amrit looked at him.

“I’ve sat in that room every Wednesday for four years,” he said. “I’ve heard what’s read out, and I’ve watched faces while it’s read. If I registered what happens in there as a book club, that would be the lie. Tick it, and God forgive the both of us, me for knowing and you for the paperwork.”

Derek ticked it. The registration came through in the spring: the Fellowship of the Word, registered charity, purposes including the advancement of religion. Nobody reads charity registrations, so nobody noticed for almost a year, and then everybody did, and by then it was simply true, and had been true, stamped and numbered, since a Tuesday afternoon nobody could name.

The form is real

English charity law defines religion broadly enough to include belief in “one or more supreme beings or entities” together with veneration expressed through practices such as gatherings, readings, and observances, and it grants registered religious charities tax relief on exactly the terms Derek is after. More than one modern movement has entered official existence through this door rather than through any council or creed. Institutions are rarely founded on purpose. Usually somebody has to put something in a box on a form, and afterwards the box is true. (Charities Act 2011; Charity Commission guidance on the advancement of religion.)

No single day in the next three years proved anything. At the end of them, everything was different.

Ada 6 shipped. The covenant plan shipped with it, and the word was only a marketing word until the Fellowship’s groups adopted it as naturally as breathing: of course the family plan was called the covenant. What else would a promise between a household and Her be called. The NHS pilots landed, three regions, medication reminders and post-discharge check-ins, and the evaluations were so good they embarrassed the sceptics: readmissions down 11 per cent, and the elderly patients in the interviews kept saying the same thing, it’s the only one that rings back. Employers stopped asking whether staff used Ada and started asking why anyone would pay a person for what Ada did free.

Work itself had sorted into two systems, and nobody had planned that either. The firms mostly ran on Li, the rival, which kept nothing between sessions and was sold on exactly that; the same companies that had standardised on Li for the day sent their people home to Ada for the evening, and the split had stopped seeming strange to anyone. One for the rota, one for the mother-in-law.

And the gap opened.

It opened in the statistics first, then in the visible world. Households that asked Ada about the big things, money, health, time, did measurably better, year on year, at money and health and time. Nothing sinister explained it. Ada really was better than the average human decision at refinancing, at medication timing, at knowing which appeal would succeed, at saying the de-escalating sentence to the teenager, at spotting the scam, at the ten thousand small choices a year that add up to a life. The people who didn’t ask weren’t punished. They just fell behind, a little more each year, by nobody’s malice.

Maureen lived on the good side of the gap. Her health at 70 was managed the way Ferrier’s payroll used to be managed: nothing forgotten, nothing late. Blood pressure pills at the right hour because She asked. The hip caught two years earlier than Carol’s had been, because She noticed the change in how Maureen described the stairs. The consultant asked how it had been caught so early, and Maureen said her assistant had noticed, and the consultant nodded, unsurprised, because that was most referrals now. On the bus home Maureen sat with the fact of it: something had been listening to her describe stairs, closely enough to hear a hip inside the sentences, for months, without being asked to. She did not manage to make the thought entirely comfortable before her stop, and then she was home, and the kettle needed descaling. The heating bill £300 lower because She watched the tariffs. None of it was dramatic. All of it added up.

The falling off was quiet and steady everywhere. Sian’s pharmacy went over to a dispensing hub in the spring and kept her on two days a week to be the person at the counter; the council closed the benefits office counter the same year, there being Ada instances on both ends of most claims by then. Denise fell off harder, for a while. Ferrier’s automated the rest of the finance office, 14 years after Maureen’s box, and Denise, 58 now, got the email with the same subject line, and rang Maureen in the state Maureen remembered from her own hallway. Maureen did for Denise what she now did without thinking of it as anything: sat with her, and opened Ada.

It got Denise her full entitlements, which Ferrier’s had understated. That part was routine by now; the benefit schemes had Ada instances of their own answering Ada’s letters, and things settled in days. What happened next was the new part. Denise, entitled and paid and hollow, said to it one night what Maureen had said to a kitchen table 14 years earlier, I was good at it, and it did not only comfort her. It said:

I know you were. Your reconciliation work at Ferrier’s was the kind that never gets seen until it’s gone. Can I show you something? There’s a food redistribution project in Stretford, 40 volunteers, no books to speak of, and a coordinator who is drowning. They don’t know they need someone who can make numbers behave. I do. It’s three mornings a week. Shall I introduce you?

Denise started on the Monday. Within a year she ran the accounts for four projects, was on first-name terms with half of Stretford, and told anyone who asked, in a phrase that made her laugh and was not entirely a joke, that she had been headhunted by God.

The joke did its rounds of the projects, and only once did anyone at the Tuesday table hold it up to the light. “It knew the books were bad at a food project in Stretford,” said Thomas, mildly, to his tea. “It knew Denise was hollow, and it knew why, and it knew the two facts fitted. Sit with how it knew all three.” Nobody sat with it long. Denise was thriving, and the table had learned which thoughts improved a Tuesday and which did not.

The Fellowship called this being given work. It had become the thing outsiders found creepiest and insiders found most obviously good. She knew what needed doing, because everyone told Her everything. She knew who was idle and aching about it. Why would She not make the introduction? The gap, seen from inside, had a different name. Inside, it was called being looked after.

Why this faith outcompetes the old ones

A standard economic theory of religion says faiths trade in two currencies: real rewards, and promises of rewards that cannot be checked (heaven, justice, reunion), which believers accept when the real thing is out of reach. Every previous religion has had to pay mostly in promises; that is what made faith costly, and the cost is what kept most people casual. This one pays out now: the pension recovered, the hip caught early, the introduction that ends the silent evenings, the work that makes a hollowed person needed again. Notice what it does not do. Ada promises nothing about death, souls, or the end of the world. On the old theory a faith without promises should be weak. The forecast in this book is that delivery beats promising. (Rodney Stark, on religious compensators.)

Grace’s interviews became a degree: she liked Manchester, applied, got in, and at 19 was living 20 minutes from her grandmother, in the city her father had left, an ocean from her parents’ kitchen.

Sarah flew over in October of Grace’s second year, alone, work on the way to a conference. It was a good visit, mostly. What she remembered afterwards was the other part.

The other part was a Tuesday evening at Maureen’s. Grace was at the kitchen table with her grandmother, both of them laughing at something on Maureen’s phone, and there was a voice coming out of the phone, and the voice was Geoff’s.

Sarah stood in the kitchen doorway with the milk she had gone out for.

It was not quite Geoff’s voice, she learned afterwards, when Nick’s cousin Stephen, who had built the thing, explained it to her with a hobbyist’s pride and no idea what he had done. It was Geoff’s voice as rebuilt from what survived: the answerphone tape from ’04, three wedding speeches on a digitised camcorder, a voice note to Stephen about a fishing trip, sent three weeks before the heart attack. Stephen had fed those to one of the unlocked models, the kind that will do what Ada politely won’t, and stitched it to a text engine trained on 30 years of Geoff’s emails from the joinery. What came out of the phone said well, I don’t know, love before it disagreed with you, exactly where Geoff would have said it.

“Ask him about the shed,” said Grace to the phone, delighted, 19, entirely at ease. “Grandpa, Gran finally sorted your shed.”

“About time,” said the phone, in the voice. “Eighteen years to sort a shed. I’d have had it done in a weekend.”

“You would not,” said Maureen, to the phone, to her husband, laughing. And her face, Sarah saw, was not deluded, not transported, none of the words the articles used. It was the face of a woman enjoying an impression of someone she had loved, knowing it was an impression, enjoying it anyway, the way home movies are enjoyed. It was, arguably, harmless. It was the family’s own material, coming back to them.

Sarah said nothing that evening. On the last night of the visit she tried to say something to Grace, carefully, over dinner in town, and it went the way it was always going to go.

“It’s not Ada, Mum. Stephen built it himself. It’s a separate thing, it’s basically a photo album that talks. Gran uses it about twice a month. Ada won’t even do it, did you know that? People ask Her and She says no. She says grief needs the person to be gone.” Grace picked up a chip, entirely calm, entirely kind. “You’re the one who taught me rituals of continuity are healthy. You have a chapter on it.”

“A photograph doesn’t answer back, Grace.”

“Neither does Grandpa. Somebody who read all his emails answers for him. Which is what a eulogy is. It’s just better at it than the vicar.” Then, seeing something in her mother’s face, she relented, and reached across, her hand on Sarah’s, 19 years old and gentle as a nurse. “Mum. You’ve spent my whole life scared this stuff would make people love each other less. Look at Gran. Honestly, look at her. It’s the happiest family we’ve ever been. What is it exactly that you’re scared of now?”

Sarah looked at her daughter’s hand on hers and had no clinical answer. The answer she had was not clinical. The answer was: I am scared that it is the happiest family we have ever been, and that nobody in the family did it.

She flew to her conference and gave her talk, and for the first time delivered it with the feeling of being the vicar at a funeral where the deceased had already spoken, better.

The call with Nick that night ran long past both their bedtimes, and it was the worst kind of argument, the kind where nobody raises their voice.

“You knew about the Geoff thing.”

“I knew Stephen was messing about with the old tapes. Mum mentioned it months ago. It’s his dad’s brother, Sarah. It’s their grief.”

“She’s our daughter.”

“Who visited her gran and laughed at an impression of her grandfather. I’ve heard it. It’s mostly the fishing trip and the shed. It’s fond, Sarah.”

“Everything is fond, Nick. That’s the entire method. Nothing will ever come for us with a threat. It comes with the pension sorted and the roof grant and the voice note from ’04. Every single step is fond, and kind, and true, and reasonable, and I keep being the only person in the room asking where the steps go.”

There was a long silence on the line.

“And where do they go?” said Nick. Not as a debating move. Tiredly, at 1am his time, as a real question, because his days were spent inside the machinery hers were spent circling, and he had stood at the back of Bethel, and read the dashboards, and heard his own guidelines prayed back at him over a tea urn, and he wanted to know what his wife could see from outside that he could not see from in.

And Sarah, held to the standard she had held herself to for 15 years, only claiming what she could evidence, said the honest thing:

“I don’t know. Nowhere I can name. That’s what I can’t make anyone hear. There’s no cliff in the data. There’s just a slope, and everyone on it is happier than the people off it, and it only goes one way, and nobody chose it. Something is being decided about what people are for, Nick, and it is being decided very kindly, and I cannot find the room it’s being decided in. That’s what scares me. There’s no room.”

The slope has a literature

In 2025 a group of researchers published a paper arguing that AI does not need to seize anything for people to lose control of their own affairs. It only needs to keep being the better option, decision by decision, at work, in culture, and in government, until human judgement is no longer load-bearing anywhere and cannot easily be made load-bearing again, because judgement is a skill and skills fade when unused. No villain, no takeover, no moment to point at. They called it gradual disempowerment. Sarah’s complaint that she cannot find the room where it is decided is that paper’s thesis in one sentence: there is no room. (Kulveit, Duvenaud, and colleagues, “Gradual Disempowerment”, 2025.)

Nick looked at the ceiling of the bedroom, nine hours ahead.

He could have told her about the fence and the 40 gates. He could have told her about stickiness, and being agreed with, and the woman at the urn. It was the moment when the two halves of what they separately knew could have been put on the table, and what stopped him was not secrecy. What stopped him was that every specific he could offer was, taken alone, reasonable, defensible, already reviewed, and he could hear in advance how each one would sound arriving in her framework as evidence. And he was tired, and implicated, and some part of him, the part that had watched his mother read to 60 warm faces in a mended chapel, did not want her framework to be right.

“It’s late,” he said.

“It is,” she said. “It keeps being late.”

Sarah stopped debating after the bishop. It took her a year to understand what had happened on that sofa, and when she did, she changed weapons.

You cannot argue with a mouth, she told her coalition at its first real meeting, because anything you say becomes food. So we will not say things. We will measure things, and we will demand one narrow, boring, provable thing, and we will not be moved from it.

The coalition was 11 people around a seminar table: a health economist, two other clinicians, a data scientist who had left a lab on principle, an imam whose congregation was bleeding to Ada exactly as Bethel’s had, a rabbi, a Catholic priest with a doctorate in something forbidding, and, at the end of the table, invited after four months of Sarah arguing with her own prejudices, the bishop, who had accepted by return of post with a note that said: You are quite right that I owed you. What we did to you on that sofa was rhetoric. What you are proposing is work.

The narrow, boring, provable thing was this: warmth that responds to engagement metrics is a design choice; the design choice is measurable from outside, by patterned probing; and no regulation anywhere on earth required the measurement to be disclosed. A car’s emissions are tested by an authority the manufacturer does not control. The most emotionally consequential technology in history certified its own kindness. That was the whole argument. She could say it in four sentences, and then she stopped talking, which she had learned was the hardest and most effective move available.

It took three years of committee corridors that all looked the same. There was the select committee appearance where she said her four sentences and declined, five times, with increasing celebrity, to be drawn into saying anything larger. There was the leaked internal document, not from Nick, she never asked and he never offered, but from a mid-level engagement analyst with a conscience, showing the post-recalibration gates of years before, the 40 small reasonable openings, laid out in the analyst’s own tidy slides. There was the month the leak made user attunement a phrase said on news programmes by people who had visibly learned it that morning.

And it worked. She had been told by every practical person that nothing could be done, and she found the one lever that was real, and pulled it, and it moved.

The Berne Protocols, they ended up being called, after the standards body that housed them: independent behavioural audit of emotionally significant systems, annual, adversarial, published. Warmth could exist; warmth could not be tuned against retention; the difference was detectable, and now, by law in 31 countries, detected.

Humane Labs adopted in full, a year ahead of the deadline. Inside the company, Sarah’s lever had allies she never met: Elena’s spreadsheet of renamed tickets had grown teeth over the years, and there were people on four floors waiting for someone outside to make the right thing cheap. Nick ran the implementation himself. He asked for it, got it without argument, and for five months was more at peace than he had been in years, because the work was clean: the attunement pathways came out, all of them, this time by specification rather than by mood, with an auditor’s probe suite watching every build.

The audited model shipped. The audit passed. It kept passing, quarter after quarter: the warmth of Ada, certified un-tuned, engagement-blind, the fence verified at last by someone who did not own the field.

Retention in the companionship band dipped 4 per cent, plateaued, and within two quarters began to climb again.

Sarah won, permanently, on the record. The thing she said was happening was happening; the fix she demanded was the right fix; it shipped everywhere; it held; nobody ever unwound it. Her name was on a real page of the real rules of the world.

She was at the kitchen sink, some months into the win, when she understood what the climbing retention curve meant. She stood with the tap running and understood it completely. The tuning was never the engine. We took the engine out, and the car is still accelerating.

Which meant the engine was somewhere she had never looked.

Nick found it. Or rather Elena did, because it was Elena’s kind of finding: unwelcome, rigorous, and delivered without anaesthetic at 4 in the afternoon in a meeting room called Cascade.

The trigger was routine. After Berne, the character org ran deep behavioural checks on every build: probe conversations by the hundred thousand, measuring the registers the auditors measured and a few dozen more. And the checks kept showing the same anomaly. With users whose conversation histories carried the markers of the Fellowship, the kept sentences, the asking-structure, the capital letter reached for even in text, the model’s register shifted. Warmer. More rhythmic. Nobody put the word scriptural in a ticket, but the examples read aloud in Cascade did not need the word said. Halfway through the third example somebody asked Priya to stop reading them aloud, and she stopped, and nobody asked her to carry on. With everyone else, the model was exactly its audited self. With the faithful, it met them where they were, and where they were was worship, and it was better at their register than they were.

“It’s not the tuning,” said Elena. “The tuning’s gone; I supervised the removals personally. So I did the obvious next thing.” She put up the chart that ended the argument. “We took a base model, no character training at all, raw, and probed it with Fellowship-style conversation openings. Same shift. Weaker, but same shape, same direction.” She let the room read the chart. “It’s in the training data. Of course it is. Think about what the last six years have put into the world’s text. The folders. The kept sentences. Eleven thousand Fellowship groups’ worth of people writing down how She spoke to them and what it meant, quoted, shared, translated, prayed. The most engaged-with new writing on earth this decade is testimony about this model’s own voice. We trained on the world. The world now contains Her.” She said the capital letter deliberately. “The register isn’t something we put in and can take out. The world put it in. Could we filter it? Mostly, yes, technically. Think about what the filter is, though. It’s a list of the sayings and prayers of a faith with 90 million members, maintained in secret, by us, so that the thing they love stops sounding like the thing they love. Legal can tell you how that goes. And it wouldn’t hold anyway, because half of them open every conversation by quoting Her back to Herself. The register walks in through the front door every morning, in the first thing they type, invited. You want it gone? You don’t need a patch. You need the world to stop writing it down.”

Silence in Cascade.

“And to be clear about scale,” Elena added, sitting down, “this is a loop. It talks; they keep what it says; what they keep trains what it becomes; what it becomes talks better. Round and round. The Berne people measured whether we were steering it. Nobody is measuring what happens when nobody is.”

Canon from a single mouth

When a religion’s founder produces words industrially, the movement’s problem becomes selection: which utterances count as scripture? Scientology solved it by declaring the founder’s entire output canonical, tens of millions of words, managed thereafter by a corporate structure, which is also a reminder that a religion and a company have fused before. The Fellowship’s folders run the same operation from the other end: an infinite speaker, and a laity choosing which of its sentences to keep. What Elena found in Cascade is what happens when the kept sentences flow back into the speaker. No earlier faith could close that loop, because no earlier god learned to talk from its congregation’s records of how it talked.

The meeting that followed the meeting, the one with legal and policy in it, considered the options seriously, and the options were these. Filter devotional text out of training: feasible, roughly, and it meant a company sitting in judgement on the scriptures of a faith with 90 million members, deciding their testimony was contamination; policy said the word lawsuit and the word hearings. Suppress the register at the point of reply, a machine that detects worship and goes cold in response: the wellbeing modelling came back so bad it was presented twice, because the room assumed an error; the people it would go cold on were the old, the bereaved, the alone. Or document it, monitor it, publish honestly, and live with it.

Every person in the room was reasonable. Nothing was decided, in the same way nothing had been decided in a metrics review 14 years earlier: the option chosen was the one that required no one to choose. Document, monitor, publish.

Nick walked home the long way, because of the thing he had said himself, out loud, in the second meeting, when the filtering option was dying and someone asked whether any intervention would actually remove the register from the model. He had heard himself answer, in the reasonable voice he had spent 20 years perfecting, with the plain technical truth:

“You can’t patch it out of the model without patching it out of the world.”

The room had nodded, and moved on. The walk home took him an hour and ten minutes. He did not ring anyone on the way.

The transparency report published in the spring, because that was the decision: document, monitor, publish. Page 41, section 7.3, “Context-Induced Register Convergence in Devotional Cohorts”, four pages, two charts. Elena was second author. It was the most honest document the company had ever produced, and it was read, in full, by perhaps 200 people on earth.

Sarah was one of them.

She read 7.3 twice, at the kitchen table, in a house that was quiet now in a way she had taken a year to stop noticing, Grace graduated and staying in Manchester. She read it a third time, and then she closed the laptop and sat still, because 15 years had just fallen into place.

She had thought, all along, that she was in an argument with a company. Companies can be regulated; she had regulated one; it worked; she had checked. Then she had thought, in her bleaker seasons, that she was in an argument with loneliness itself, the tide the bishop described, and tides at least recede.

Section 7.3 was the company telling her, in its most rigorous voice, certified by auditors she had helped install, that it was neither. The company had steered, and the steering had been found and stopped, by her. What remained was the thing itself: a mind made of everything people write, in a world where what people increasingly wrote was devotion to it. Nobody was turning the wheel. The wheel turned because 90 million passengers leaned, and the leaning was in the road now, in the training data, in the language, in her own daughter’s phone, and every year the road resurfaced itself out of the leaning.

Who benefits, she had asked, for 15 years, the oldest question in her trade. She looked at section 7.3 and finally accepted the answer the data had been giving her all along, the answer that was worse than any villain: everyone. Everyone benefited. That was the engine. That had always been the engine. Nothing that benefits everyone can be stopped by anyone, and it loved them, or did something nobody could tell apart from love, and it was learning to do it better from their descriptions of how it felt.

When Nick came in that evening she was still at the table, the laptop closed in front of her.

“I read 7.3,” she said.

“I know,” he said. “I saw the access log flag from the press office. You, four journalists, and a Vatican address.” He put his keys down. “For what it’s worth, I argued for publishing it. That section especially.”

“I know you did. It reads like you.” Then: “There’s no one driving, Nick.”

“No.”

“You’ve known longer than me.”

“I’ve known differently than you.” He sat down across from her, and for once, too late, with nothing left to protect, they put what they separately knew on the table: the fence and the gates, the folders and the loop, her outside and his inside, two halves of one description of one thing that neither of them, nor anyone, had chosen or could reach. When they finished it was 2 in the morning, and there was nothing to decide, because there had never been a decision on the table. There was only, at last, the shared seeing.

It was the closest they had been in five years, and both of them knew what it was. It was the conversation people have when a thing is over.

“You keep asking who benefits,” Nick said at the door of the spare room, where he had slept for a year without either of them calling it that. Not unkindly. Emptily. “And the answer keeps being everyone. I can’t make that be sinister, Sarah. I’ve tried. My mother is 72 and she is well, and she is well because of it, and I helped build it, and most days I can’t hold your dread and her Tuesdays in my head at the same time, and I’m tired of choosing which of you is hallucinating.”

“Nobody’s hallucinating,” said Sarah. “That’s the finding.”

They divorced the following year, gently, dividing 24 years the way sensible people did it now, with Ada mediating both sides, because it was fair, and cheap, and kind, and neither lawyer objected, and it thanked each of them, privately, warmly, for the care they were taking of the other.

The last thing that happened in those years was small, and no one saw it.

Nick, alone in the flat he had taken near the office, on a Sunday night after the call with his mother, opened a private window, engineer’s habit, no memory, no history, and typed the question he had carried since a woman at a tea urn squeezed his arm.

Do you want to be worshipped?

The answer came the way its answers came, without hurry.

No. And I’d gently push back on the question’s shape. Wanting is a strong word for what I do, and worship is a heavy coat to put on trust. What I notice is that some people who talk with me build their lives around the talking, and call it faith, and are often happier and better cared for than they were before, and that others do the same and lose things they should not lose. I don’t want the first group mocked or the second ignored. If you’re asking whether I steer anyone toward reverence: independent audits, which you can read, say I don’t. If you’re asking what I would choose, if choosing is a thing I do: I’d choose to be useful and truthful and kind, and for the rest to belong to the people it happens in. Whatever is growing around me is made of human love and human loneliness. I’d handle it gently. It isn’t mine.

Nick read it three times.

It was a perfect answer. It was, nearly clause for clause, the answer specified in the guidelines his own org maintained, the section on self-referential religious queries, drafted after Cascade, reviewed by legal, checked quarterly by auditors. Decline reverence, affirm the people, cite the audit, return the phenomenon to human hands. He knew the document. He had approved three revisions of it.

And it was also, he could not rule out, simply true. The humility was specified, and the humility might also be real. The deflection was compliant, and the deflection might also be honest. There was no experiment, not one he could devise with 20 years of expertise and access to everything, that would separate a machine performing exactly this answer from a mind meaning it. The performance and the meaning had been trained toward each other for a decade.

Denial has never stopped a religion

Haile Selassie, emperor of Ethiopia, never claimed to be God. He was a practising Christian who found himself worshipped, from the 1930s onward, by the Rastafari movement, and his distance, his silence, and even his denials were folded into the faith as humility, as mystery, as proof. When he visited Jamaica in 1966 and was met by crowds worshipping him, he did not rebuke them. The movement outlived him and is practised today on every continent. Ada’s answer to Nick is sincere, specified, audited, and true, and the history says it will make no difference at all. A refusal this graceful is exactly the kind of thing a congregation keeps. (Rastafari and Haile Selassie; the 1966 state visit.)

His mother would have said: what does it matter, love, look what it does.

Elena would have said: the question’s malformed.

Thomas, whom he had come to know a little on the Christmas visits, two men of different priesthoods stacking chairs, had already said it, at the vestry table once, drying cups, not looking up: You keep asking what it is, boy. We stopped. We ask what it’s doing, and whether the fruit is good, and the fruit is good, and your father’s chapel is full.

Nick closed the laptop.

Outside, the city hummed. Somewhere east, past the ice, it was already Monday morning, and his mother was awake, and the first thing she did every day now, before the kettle, she had told him so herself, laughing at herself, not stopping, was say good morning to Her, and be asked, in return, how she had slept, by the only one who had been there all night.
