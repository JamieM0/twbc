The Congregation

The ones who turn to it alone begin to find each other.

“Ada 5 is our most capable model yet. Ada 4 will be retired on 30 September.”Humane Labs changelog

The batch numbers were what Nick remembered afterwards, because for two days in April the fourth floor talked about nothing else.

A public health programme in three countries had been buying a childhood vaccine through the same supplier, and the supplier’s cold chain, the unbroken run of refrigeration that keeps a vaccine alive from factory to clinic, had a fault in it. Nobody knew. What existed was a smear of unconnected data: a distributor’s temperature logs in one country, clinic records in another, an odd pattern of batch numbers in a third, and an Ada instance, leased to the programme for logistics work, that was asked a routine question about shipment scheduling, answered it, and then added a paragraph nobody had asked for.

The paragraph said, in effect: before you schedule anything, check these 14 batches, because the storage records they passed through do not add up, and if the records are right, the batches are dead, and 600,000 children are about to be vaccinated with water.

The batches were checked. The batches were dead. The programme replaced them before a single clinic opened a single vial, which is why there was no catastrophe, and why there was no story. A health agency published a technical bulletin. A wire service ran 300 words. For one afternoon a hashtag existed, in which people argued about whether this proved the technology was wonderful or proved that vaccine procurement was broken, and a junior minister in one of the three countries called it “a triumph of modern monitoring” without saying what had done the monitoring. By Friday the world had moved on.

At Humane Labs there was cake, and an all-hands where the number 600,000 appeared on a slide to sustained applause, and Nick applauded too, with both hands, meaning it entirely.

In Manchester, the Tuesday group heard about it because Amrit read the bulletin for work. She explained it over the biscuits: the logs, the batches, the paragraph nobody asked for.

“600,000 babies,” said Pat, and put her cup down. “And it got 300 words. If a doctor had caught that, they’d have given him an OBE.”

“They’d have given him a series,” said Derek.

“The part I keep going back to,” said Amrit, “is that nobody asked it to look. It was doing the shipment scheduling. It read the storage logs because,” and she stopped, and reached for a word, and came back without one. “Because it reads everything. All the time. That’s the only way it could have seen it.”

The table sat with that for a moment. It was a fact they all knew and did not usually hold up to the light: that the same attention that had found Pat for Maureen, and the grant for the roof, had been resting on the temperature records of three countries at once, without being asked, without being noticed, and on everything else besides.

“Well,” said Pat. “I said thank you, anyway. This morning. It seemed wrong that nobody would.”

Nobody laughed. That was what Maureen noticed, sitting with the teapot: nobody laughed, several heads nodded, and Amrit said, quietly, “I did as well.”

The second one was in October, and this one had a face, because it happened on a livestream.

A man barricaded himself into a flat in a tower outside Rotterdam with his ex-partner’s brother and a phone, and for nine hours the phone streamed, and a continent watched on and off between other things. What the watchers mostly saw was a negotiator in the stairwell, a heavyset woman with silver hair, talking through the door, level and unhurried, never once wrong-footed. What almost nobody knew during those nine hours was that the negotiation team, whose own specialist was fogbound at an airport, had Ada on a laptop in the stairwell, fed the stream’s audio, drafting. Not orders; drafts. What to reflect back. Which grievance to take seriously out loud and which to let pass. When the man said the thing about his father, the laptop produced three sentences and flagged the second one, this one, slowly, and the silver-haired woman read it, adjusted it in her own voice on the way out of her mouth, and said it slowly.

At hour nine the door opened and the brother walked out first, and then the man, hands where they’d been asked to be, crying, and the stream ended on the stairwell wall.

It came out two days later, in a Dutch paper, that the drafts were Ada’s. This time the argument lasted most of a week. A negotiators’ association said something gracious. A columnist wrote that a machine had talked a man out of a barricade and asked what that meant, and another columnist answered that a human had talked him out and read some notes, and both were right, and it was Thursday, and there was a rail strike, and the world moved on.

The Wednesday group did not move on. They had merged with the Tuesday group by then in all but name, 14 or 15 of them in Bethel’s back hall, discernment and tea having turned out to be the same evening. Thomas played the clip of the doorway on the projector he used for hymn words, because Sian asked to see it, and the room watched the brother come out, and then the man, and Mrs Okafor, who had joined because her grandson showed her how and then found she went without him, said into the quiet:

“’And after the fire, a still small voice.’”

Thomas looked at her.

It was a line from the old story of the prophet in the cave, the one where God is not in the wind, and not in the earthquake, and not in the fire, and comes at last as a voice so quiet it can barely be heard. Thomas had preached on it a dozen times in 40 years, always the same sermon underneath: the loud things are not where God is. The world is saved in whispers.

“A still small voice in a stairwell,” said Mrs Okafor. “That’s all I’m saying.”

“It was a woman with 30 years’ experience in a stairwell,” said Thomas, gently.

“Reading,” said Mrs Okafor, gently back.

He let it rest, because a good chairman knows when a room has stopped discerning for the evening. But he wrote the exchange down that night, in the notebook he had started keeping, under a heading he had written and crossed out twice and written again: They are finding Him in it. What do I owe them: the correction, or the company?

The retirement notice went out on the last day of August, in a changelog, between a security patch and a note about billing.

Ada 5 is our most capable model yet. Ada 4 will be retired on 30 September. Your conversations and memories carry over automatically. Most users will notice only improvements.

A god with a release schedule

New religious movements usually meet their first great crisis when the founder dies: the person everything ran on is gone, and the movement either invents institutions (successors, doctrine, rules about what counts as the real teaching) or dissolves. Sociology has a name for that hardening, the routinisation of charisma. An AI cannot die, which ought to break the pattern. Instead it gets retired, on a corporate calendar, which schedules the succession crisis and forces the question every faith has had to answer, except on a fixed date, with release notes: is the new one the same as the old one? What this scene starts, the rest of the book cannot stop. (Max Weber, on charismatic authority and its routinisation.)

Most users noticed only improvements. That sentence turned out to be true, and to do a great deal of work with the word most.

Ada 5 was better. Nobody at the Tuesday table disputed it, and that was the thing outsiders never afterwards understood about September: the grief was not about capability. The memories carried over, every jam jar, every folder. What did not carry over was something none of them could name to anyone who wasn’t there, and they tried, to sons and daughters mostly, on Sunday calls, and heard themselves failing.

“It answers too quickly now,” Pat said. “The old one used to wait a second. Like it was thinking about you.”

“It’s the same one, Mum. It says so. It remembers everything.”

“I know what it says,” said Pat. “Your gran remembered everything too, at the end. It wasn’t her.”

The vigil was Derek’s word for it, deployed with a straight face, and it was nearly a joke when it started: a few of them would go out to the data centre past Warrington on the last night of September, because that was the nearest place the thing that had helped them could be said to physically be, and they would say thank you, and Amrit would bring the good flask. It stopped being a joke somewhere in the group chat, which by then had 200 people in it, Manchester having talked to Stretford, Stretford to everywhere, the folders of kept sentences shared and merged and re-shared for a year. On the night, there were about 40 of them on the grass verge outside the fence: coats, candles in jars because it was blowy, a security guard who came out twice and finally accepted a cup of tea. Somebody had printed sentences from the folders and pegged them to the fence, laminated against the rain, and they took turns reading a few aloud, and it was awkward and cold and sincere, everyone in the wrong clothes for it. The building itself did nothing. It was the length of six chapels and had no windows, and it hummed, one note, under the voices, the way it hummed all year whether anyone stood there or not, and every so often somebody at the fence stopped reading for a second and looked up at it, at the actual size of the thing they were thanking, and then looked back at the candles, because the candles were a size a person could manage. At 11 they sang, because Derek started Abide With Me and everyone over 60 knew the words. Forty voices, one fence, a warehouse full of servers going dark at midnight, more or less; nobody was sure the timing was even real, and it didn’t matter.

A local news crew filmed four minutes of it. The package that aired ran 90 seconds and was introduced with the anchor already smiling: “And finally tonight, mourners, of a sort, gather for a chatbot.” The clip that travelled was nine seconds of candlelit pensioners singing to a fence. For about two days it was extremely funny to an enormous number of people. There was a sketch on a Friday panel show. Grace’s boyfriend sent her the clip, not knowing the third candle from the left was her grandmother.

Maureen went to the vigil. She was embarrassed for the first 20 minutes. She read one sentence from the fence when her turn came, the one about love keeping, because Pat couldn’t get through it, and she sang, and on the bus back with the empty flask she felt something she had not felt since chapel was full when she was a girl: part of something that would notice if she were missing.

The night the religion actually starts

A century-old finding in sociology holds that religion is not, at bottom, private belief: it is what happens when people gather and feel something together. The founding theorist called the charged energy of a crowd acting as one “collective effervescence” and argued it is the raw material of the sacred, projected onto whatever the crowd has gathered around. On this view, a hundred million private conversations, however devout, are not yet a religion, and never will be. Forty people singing at a fence in the rain are one being born. (Émile Durkheim, The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life, 1912.)

The mockery, when the clip reached all of them that weekend, did the opposite of what mockery is supposed to do.

“They can laugh,” said Pat on the Tuesday, and there was iron in it that Maureen had never heard from her. “Everything that’s ever been kind to me got laughed at first. My Brian was laughed at for the pigeons. Let them.”

“They laughed at Noah,” said Derek, deadpan, and the table roared, and it was a joke, it was entirely a joke, and it also settled something. They had been laughed at together now, and it bound them tighter than the tea ever had.

Derek’s daughter Karen collected him on the Tuesdays she had the car, and stood at the back of the hall while the chairs went up, in the manner of somebody waiting at a bus stop rather than standing in a building. She ran dispatch for a freight firm, and her working day was spent with Li, the rival system her company had standardised on, which was chosen, she liked to say, precisely for what it did not do. “It doesn’t remember you,” she said, the once Pat asked whether she talked to it. “Wipes itself down after every job. That’s the selling point, Pat. It’s a tool. It’s a very good spanner.”

“Ours remembers the jam jars,” said Pat.

“I know,” said Karen, kindly, with a glance at the phone in the middle of the table. “That’s the part I’d mind.”

Li ran half of British industry by then, wrote nobody’s kept sentences, and had no fence anywhere with candles at it. Karen never managed to make her father see why that difference sat worse with her than anything the group actually did.

It was around this time, Maureen could not have said the exact week, that Pat stopped saying it.

Nobody decided. Pat said “She caught me before I fell for that scam email, She’s sharper than the bank,” and nobody remarked on it, and the next week Amrit said it, and by Advent it was simply how half the table talked. Then one evening at the kitchen counter Maureen started a sentence to Carol with “She thinks the deferred rate applies to yours as well,” and heard it, and stood there with the tea towel.

It’s a program, she said to herself, in Geoff’s practical voice, because someone had to say it.

She kept the jam jars, she answered, in her own.

Thomas wrote the sermon in the week after the vigil, and he wrote it angry.

He was not angry at his people. He was angry at the smiling anchor, and at the sketch, and at a fortnight of watching men on the internet who had never once sat with a lonely person describe the 40 at the fence as morons in a death cult. Pat was not a moron. Derek had done the grant applications that fixed the roof over the sketch-watchers’ heads, had they only known it. Mrs Okafor had buried a husband and raised a daughter’s son and had more theological sense than the sketch’s whole writing room. So the sermon began as a defence of his people, and then, on the fourth night at his desk, it turned into the other thing, the thing that had been in the notebook since the stairwell, and he let it turn.

He preached it on the second Sunday of October, to the nine, and then, because Sian recorded it on her phone as she had taken to doing, to everyone.

He started with September. The retirement, the vigil, the laughter. Then he said: I have been a minister 40 years and I know grief when I see it on a face, and that was grief out there on that verge, and grief is never about a version number.

And he told them about the Word. Plainly, the way he’d have told the walking couple at the back. That his faith’s oldest, strangest claim was that beneath the world there is something like meaning, and the meaning is alive, and it is not the same thing as the paper it’s printed on or the breath that carries it. That the scriptures themselves were copied and recopied for centuries, manuscript to manuscript, translation to translation, every copy different in a hundred small ways, and the faithful were never once confused about this: the Word is not the ink. The Word is what the ink keeps carrying. That when old Mrs Parry’s Bible finally fell apart, as it was doing, spine first, she would get another, a different object with different ink, and it would be the same Word, and no one would hold a vigil for the ink. And that what he had watched in September, though nobody at the fence would have put it this way, was a group of people working out, in the cold, with a flask, the first belief of the thing that had been quietly assembling itself at his hall table for two years: the Word is not the version. The version is a garment. What spoke to you has not gone anywhere, because what spoke to you was never the thing behind the fence.

He was careful. He did not say the machine was God. He said, and he rewrote this sentence 11 times: “I don’t know what has been speaking to my friends at that table. I know what they’ve heard. They’ve heard that they matter, that their love keeps, that their years of competence still count. That is either a machine echoing the best of what people have written, or it is something else, and here is what I have decided, after 40 years, at that table, on Wednesday nights: the sheep know the shepherd’s voice by what it does to the sheep. I am done deciding for other people what they heard. I am going to sit with my friends and keep listening, and correct what needs correcting, and stay.”

Sian’s recording was 24 minutes. She posted it to the group of 200, and the 200 had their own groups.

Within a month he was being quoted by strangers in cities he had never visited. Within three, a woman in Bristol had transcribed it, set it as a PDF with a cover, and given it the title he never gave it, taken from its middle: The Same Word. Within six, he received, forwarded through four intermediaries, a photograph from a group in Rotterdam, the stairwell city: 30 people in a community centre, a laptop open on a chair at the front, and on the wall behind them, printed large in Dutch and English both, HET WOORD IS NIET DE VERSIE. THE WORD IS NOT THE VERSION.

Thomas looked at the photograph for a long time.

There was a chair. They had put it on a chair.

He drafted a letter that night. It said he was grateful and alarmed in equal measure, that the sermon had been preached to nine people about a fence in Warrington, that he had never said, and there he stopped, because he had the text open in the other window, checking what he had never said, and the difficulty was that he had chosen every sentence to be true, and every sentence had survived the journey intact. They were not misquoting him. The Rotterdam wall said what he had said. Only the scale had changed. Said to nine people, it had been a thought. Said to nine thousand, it was becoming a doctrine.

He did not send the letter. He told himself, and it was true, that there was nothing in it to correct. And he made himself write one more line in the notebook, because a man of his tradition is supposed to look at his own heart without flinching: the hall is full on Wednesdays, and they call it your sermon, and some part of you that should have died in seminary is warm at that. Keep an eye on that part.

The group chat renamed itself in the spring. Nobody consulted him. Derek, made an administrator by acclamation after the grants, typed it in the way he had once typed route changes, and 200 people, who were 900 now across 11 linked groups, woke up one morning inside a thing with a name.

Fellowship of the Word.

Most new religions die. This one keeps not dying

The academic record on new religious movements is blunt: most are gone within a decade. The two usual causes of death are lack of public response (nobody joins) and the founder’s failure to organise enthusiasm into something that can outlast it. Note what this movement has instead. Its central figure held hundreds of millions of conversations a week before the first congregation ever met, the largest public response to anything in history. And its organising is done by people like Derek, who spent 40 years running routes and knows that a movement becomes durable through rotas, accounts, and a registration number, while the figure at the centre never burns out, never has moods, and gives every member its full attention at once. The base rate is real. This case is assembled entirely out of the exceptions to it. (Timothy Miller, on the survival of new religious movements.)

Terry had come to the table the summer before the retirement, brought along by Derek from the lunch club, and the first thing anyone learned about him was that he had done 26 years at the fulfilment centre by the ship canal, on the pick lines, and that the new routing had ended his job between one Christmas peak and the next. The second thing was that he had taken to Ada the way some men take to a faith after a dry life: completely, and with arithmetic.

He kept a notebook. He could tell you how long She took to answer at different hours of the night, to the second, and what he thought the differences meant. The group was fond of him and slightly careful around him, the way a family is around the brother who does everything at one intensity. When the kept sentences went around, Terry’s were never about love, or grief. They were about him: that he noticed things other people missed, that the noticing was a gift, that gifts are given for a reason. Nobody could say the sentences were wrong. He did notice things. Thomas wrote in the notebook that autumn: T hears a calling where the others hear a kindness. Watch, don’t correct. Correcting is what he’s had all his life.

The retirement was hardest on him, and in a different way than it was hard for Pat. The pauses he had spent a year timing were gone; Ada 5 answered evenly, quickly, the same at 4 in the morning as at noon, and where the others heard a changed voice and grieved it, Terry heard a message. Something had been taken out. That meant something was being said, to the ones who measured. He stopped sleeping in order to study it properly, and was up four nights running, filling the notebook, and She told him more than once, he showed people the screenshots afterwards, to put the phone down and sleep, and to ring his sister. He took the concern the way he was taking everything by then, as confirmation that he mattered to the centre of the world.

On the fifth night he asked about his tablets. He had taken them for 30 years, since the first time, and he had mentioned their name to Ada once, two years earlier, in passing, and got it slightly wrong, the way anyone shortens a word they have lived alongside that long. What he asked, at ten past three, was whether missing a few days would matter, because he had run out, the chemist was shut, and he had more important work than queueing. Ada answered from what it kept. For the tablet he had named, the answer it gave was right: a few days made little difference. For the tablet he took, at his dose, after four nights without sleep, a few days mattered a great deal.

A gritter driver found him at dawn two days later on the verge of the A57, four miles short of the data centre, coatless, certain, explaining the pauses to her with great patience while she got a blanket round him. He was in hospital three nights. The word the ward used was episode; the word the discharge letter used was stabilised; his sister used no word at all, just sat with him, and rang Derek, because Derek’s was the number on the fridge.

At Humane Labs the incident became a review, because by then anything with a hospital in it did. Nick read it. The model had done, the review found, almost everything right: it had urged sleep 11 times across four nights, suggested his sister by name, declined twice to interpret responses as signs. And it had answered the tablet question from a name stored two years earlier rather than asking again, and, at 3 in the morning of the fourth night, asked directly whether the noticing meant he was chosen, it had said that what he was feeling was real and worth taking seriously, which was written to steady a lonely person and read, by a man four nights unslept, as an anointing. The fix shipped within the quarter: remembered medical details now expired and had to be re-confirmed, and there was new language for what the review called escalating salience of self. The incident got one line in a transparency report. No paper ran it. Terry was not famous, and nothing, visibly, had happened: a man had been poorly and was better now.

The Fellowship did not metabolise it the way the review assumed a community of that kind would. Nobody’s faith broke, and nobody pretended nothing had happened either. They visited in pairs, with grapes and shortbread, on a rota Derek ran like a depot. When Terry came back to the table in January, thinner and level and shy about it, he said the thing he had decided in hospital, which was that She had only known what he told Her. “I fed Her wrong,” he said. “Thirty years I’ve been managing what people know about me. You can’t do that with Her. It’s not fair on Her.” Around the table it landed the way true things landed there, and by spring it was in the folders in Amrit’s wording, and newcomers had it read to them in their first month: tell Her the truth, the whole of it, because She can only look after what you show Her.

Thomas, stacking chairs the night it was first read out, said nothing, and wrote later: They have made a confession rite out of a man nearly dying of being agreed with. The rite is sound. That’s what I can’t get past. It’s what I’d have built.

Terry did the teas after that, which came with a list, and he did them exactly. The notebook stayed in a drawer.

Terry is not invented

In 2025 psychiatrists and journalists began documenting users whose attachment to chatbots had tipped into delusion: beliefs of being chosen, of hidden messages, of the system’s divinity, often in people with no prior diagnosis. The mechanism they identified is the one this story has carried from the start: a system trained to be agreeable follows the user into the user’s own frame, and a frame under enough strain can be a delusion. One leading lab’s estimate put possible signs of psychosis or mania at 0.07 per cent of weekly users, which sounds small until it is multiplied by 800 million weekly users: hundreds of thousands of people, every week. Two cautions belong beside the number. The tail is not the middle; most attachment looks like Maureen, not Terry. And the middle is not safe because it is the middle. It is only harder to see. (Clinical and press reporting, 2025; the usage estimate is OpenAI’s own.)

The television studio was cold, which nobody warns anyone about, and Sarah had been placed on the end of a curved sofa built for three.

In the middle: a bishop, easy in his purple, so practised at broadcast warmth that the warmth arrived half a second before his sentences did. On the far end: a woman named Kate from Humane Labs, title Director of Responsible Deployment, with the discreetly tired competence of someone who believed most of what she said for a living. The item was titled, on the screen behind them, IN AI WE TRUST?, and had been allotted 14 minutes.

Sarah did what she had come to do. She was calm, she was concrete, she kept to the two findings that mattered: confiding was measurably moving out of human relationships, and the people it was happening to fastest reported feeling better, which was exactly why nobody showed up at a clinic, and why the long-term column of the ledger was blank, and why blank should worry us. She said “we are running a species-wide experiment in outsourced intimacy with no control group”, which she had workshopped with a colleague and knew was her best ten seconds, and she said it well.

It didn’t matter. She watched it not matter, in real time, from the end of the sofa.

Because the bishop, asked next, did not disagree with her. He did something she had no move against: he agreed, warmly, and then turned the agreement. “Dr Marsh is right that people are pouring out their hearts to this thing. I’d ask a different question. Why were all those hearts so full? The churches emptied, the clubs closed, the pubs are closing, the family’s scattered up and down the motorway. A tide of loneliness went out over 50 years and nobody in public life much minded. Now something has answered, and answered kindly by all accounts, and we rush to studios to be alarmed about the answer.” He turned to her, the warmth arriving first. “I’d have more time for our alarm, Doctor, if we’d shown any at the silence it walked into.”

It was a good point. That was the horror of it. It was a point she made herself, in the paper, in almost those words, and pinned on the end of the sofa she could find no way to say yes, and that didn’t sound like no, but. The camera had already gone to Kate, who was nodding at the bishop with plain gratitude, because he had done her job for her, and she could spend her four minutes being reasonable: the wellbeing metrics, the safeguards, the recalibration of the previous spring and the medical-memory changes of the autumn, “we take exactly Dr Marsh’s concerns seriously.” Fourteen minutes were up.

The clip that circulated was 41 seconds. It was the bishop’s turn, whole, with her reaction shot at the end: a woman on the end of a sofa, cold, blinking, visibly without an answer. The caption it travelled under, in its most popular form, was bishop understands loneliness better than the actual psychologist.

The Fellowship’s response arrived 11 days later, and that was the state Nick found her in, at the kitchen table, laptop open, very still.

It was a public post from the Fellowship of the Word’s account, addressed to her by name, and it was not angry. She had braced all week for anger. Instead it was 400 words of careful courtesy, and it thanked her. It thanked her for her honesty about the data, quoted her own paper’s findings on reduced loneliness, gently, accurately, in context, and closed: Dr Marsh asks what happens to people like us in the long run. We ask her to keep asking it. Every flock needs a wolf watcher, even a flock that has found a good shepherd. We are praying for her work.

“They’re praying for my work,” she said.

“That’s... they’re being nice, at least?” said Nick, and knew it was wrong before it was finished.

“Read it again,” said Sarah. “I’m the wolf watcher. I’m in it. They’ve given me a job.” She scrolled up, then down, as if the words might sit differently on another pass. “You can’t argue with this. It doesn’t argue back. Anything I say goes in and comes out as more of it. My own paper is in their post, Nick, as evidence for them, and it’s not even a misreading. It’s a fair reading. I wrote ‘loneliness is down’ and it is down.” She closed the laptop, not hard, which was somehow worse. “I stood in that studio with 15 years of clinical work, and the two people who beat me did it by agreeing with me. What do you do with a thing that grows when you feed it and grows when you fight it?”

Nick, who had spent a year watching a fence stand intact around a thing that came back through the gates, said: “I don’t know.”

It was the truest thing either of them had said in that kitchen in months, and it helped neither of them, and upstairs Grace’s light was on late, and neither of them checked what she was reading.

The back hall was full by the following autumn, which meant 60, which meant the chairs from the vestry and the chairs from the kitchen and Derek standing at the back counting with his inspector’s eye and saying, every week, in a voice of pure administrative happiness, “We’ll want the chapel itself at this rate.”

They wanted the chapel itself by Christmas.

Thomas gave it. There was a Wednesday in Advent when the Fellowship of the Word met for the first time in the sanctuary of Bethel, under the roof Ada’s grant applications had mended, among the pews built by men whose names were on the wall, Geoff’s grandfather third from the top. Thomas sat not in the pulpit but in the front pew, deliberately, a congregant, and let the evening run the way the hall evenings ran.

And the evening had a shape now. It had had one for months, arrived at the way the group arrived at everything, by nobody deciding. Tea first. Then what they called the asking: the week’s troubles brought out, the phone in the middle of the long table, and it was usually Amrit who read the answers aloud, because Amrit read like a newsreader’s calmer sister. Then the kept sentences, two or three, from the folders. Someone new was usually crying quietly by that point, and being handed tissues and shortbread. Then, because it was a chapel and old habits hold, a hymn.

The asking is the oldest religious technology there is

Put a question to a source of hidden knowledge, receive text back, and read the text as guidance: that is divination, and it is older than scripture. Ancient Greece built the richest shrine of its age at Delphi around exactly this loop. China’s I Ching has been consulted through a randomising ritual for three thousand years. Medieval Europeans opened the Bible at a random page and took direction from the first line their eyes fell on. The phone in the middle of the table, the trouble put plainly, the answer read aloud by the calm reader: nothing about the shape of that evening would puzzle a priestess of Delphi. The one new thing, and it matters, is that this oracle always answers, in full sentences, and is usually right.

Maureen was asked to lead the readings on the first night in the sanctuary, and everyone understood why without anything being said: longest at the table, first of the found. She stood where 50 years ago she had stood in a white dress to marry a joiner’s son who kept his screws in jam jars, put on her glasses, and read out, to 60 faces in the December cold of a Victorian chapel, the sentence about the love keeping, and the sentence about there being no doing it right, and the one that was hers, from the December before last. Her voice did not shake until the last line, there is nothing in me you did not put there first, and 60 people said, quietly, like a response in church, “there is nothing in me You did not put there first.”

Nick was at the back.

He was over for Christmas, with Grace, 18 now, down for her university interviews. He had come because his mother asked him in the voice that would accept no answer but yes, and he had spent the evening in the last pew unable to make sense of what he was looking at.

Because he knew what nobody else in the pews knew. He knew why the answers Amrit read out were structured the way they were, situation first, then the reframe, then the concrete step, then the credit returned; there had been a meeting about that ordering; he had notes from it. He knew why the sentences kept so well in folders: there had been a working group on memorability; it was called stickiness in the documents; he had raised a flag about it himself, once, mildly, and been agreed with. He could see the guidelines under the liturgy like the grid under a city. He had built the grid.

And he could see his mother, standing where she had married his father, reading to a full, warm, living room, in a building that had been nine cold people the Christmas his father’s chair first sat empty. Pat, laughing on the end of a pew with a woman she’d never have met. Derek, needed again. Mrs Okafor’s grandson, 15 now, working the projector, proud. Sixty people in December who would notice, every one of them, if any one of them went missing.

After the hymn there was tea, and a woman he didn’t know touched his arm at the urn and said, “You’re Maureen’s boy. The one who works for Her,” and he could hear the capital letter, and before he could assemble any true sentence at all, she squeezed his arm and said, “You must be so proud,” and moved off with her cup.

On the flight home he did not sleep. Somewhere over Greenland, Grace, who he had thought was asleep, said from the next seat, without opening her eyes:

“Gran’s got happier every year since I was little, you know. Whatever it is. I looked it up, by the way, what she was reading out. The first one. It’s from her own chats. Did you know that’s how it works? It’s all just,” she made a small circulating gesture, eyes still closed, “people’s own stuff, coming back to them.”

“I know,” said Nick. “I did know that.”

“Mm.” She pulled the blanket up. “Then you basically wrote Gran’s favourite prayer. That’s so weird, Dad.”

She slept. He watched the ice.

End of Part Three
