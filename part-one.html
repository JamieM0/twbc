The Tool

Everything works. Relying on it is the smart move.

“Ada held more conversations last week than any product we have ever shipped. We have begun to think of it as infrastructure for good judgement.”Humane Labs, letter to shareholders

The flag came up at 3:10 on a Friday afternoon. The week’s wages left for the bank at 4, all 900 of them, every branch, and anything flagged before then was Maureen’s to clear.

She leaned in and read it. The checking system had circled Kevin Bright, yard foreman at Trafford Park: 31 hours of overtime in one week, twice his usual pattern. Beside his name sat a small amber triangle and the words REVIEW RECOMMENDED.

She didn’t need to review it. Stocktake was last week. Alan Hodge was off having his knee replaced, and Kevin had covered the yard alone from Tuesday. The hours were real. She had done this man’s wages for 22 years, and when he rang about his payslip he still asked how she was keeping, every time, before he’d say what he wanted.

“Kevin again?” said Denise, from the other desk.

“Stocktake,” said Maureen.

She clicked OVERRIDE. The system accepted it without argument, then did the thing it had started doing after the last update: a small box opened underneath, marked REASON FOR OVERRIDE, so that it could learn.

Maureen looked at the box for a moment.

Because I know him, she typed, and the wages went out at 7 minutes to 4.

The direction this usually runs

Maureen checking the machine and being right is the arrangement working as designed. The research on people and automated systems says the arrangement rarely stays that way: as a system keeps being right, people check it less, and start to prefer its answer to their own even on the occasions it is wrong. The habit has a name, automation bias, and its quieter partner, automation complacency, is just the checking fading out because checking keeps finding nothing. (Parasuraman and Manzey, “Complacency and Bias in Human Use of Automation”, 2010.)

The office emptied in its usual order. Denise left at 4:30, calling something about the weekend from the door. Purchasing went at 5. Maureen stayed until quarter past. She liked the building quiet, and she had held her own key since 2011.

On the bus home she looked again at the photo Grace had sent during the week: her granddaughter on a sunlit American street, holding up a science fair certificate and pulling a face at it. 15 now. Maureen still hadn’t worked out what the project was. Something to do with rivers.

The house was dark when she got in, because it was November. She put the lamps, the oven, then the radio on, in the order she had kept for 18 years. It was a good life, and she’d have said so if asked. Work. The allotment. Carol on Wednesdays. The chapel now and again, for Geoff’s sake more than God’s. A full week, every week.

Sunday. 10 in the morning in San Francisco, 6 in the evening in Manchester, and Maureen’s face was propped against the fruit bowl on Nick’s kitchen island, slightly too close to her own camera, the top of the dresser behind her.

“So the quiz night’s the 23rd,” she said. “We’re doing it for the roof. Thomas has been up in the loft with a torch and he says you can see clear through to next door’s aerial.”

“That sounds structural, Mum.”

“It’s a chapel roof, love, it’s been structural since the war. We need £4,000 and we have a biscuit tin with about £90 in it. I’ve told him I’ll do the books and Eleri’s doing the raffle.”

Grace drifted through behind Nick with her headphones around her neck and leaned into the frame. “Hi, Gran.”

“Hello, my darling. What was the river project? Your dad couldn’t explain it.”

“It was sediment. Dad, I told you twice.”

“She told me twice,” said Nick.

Grace took a banana off the island, from directly beneath her grandmother’s chin, and drifted out again. Sarah slid onto the stool next to Nick with her coffee. She had been half in and half out of the call from the sink.

“How’s the hip, Maureen? Did Carol have her scan?”

“Tuesday. She’s convinced it’s the end of days, but she was convinced of that about her wisdom teeth.” Maureen waved a hand at something off-screen. “I’ve onions in, by the way. First time. The man two plots over says November’s right, and I’ve decided to believe him.”

“And work’s okay?” said Nick. He asked it a little too lightly. His company’s name had started appearing in the same headlines as words like restructure, and he had never found a place to put the fact that his mother processed payroll and he did what he did.

“Work’s fine. They’ve a new checker on the system, very keen, flags everything that moves. But it can’t do the exceptions. It doesn’t know anybody.” She said it cheerfully. “So I’m safe as houses, love. Somebody has to know why.”

After the call ended, the kitchen was quiet. Nick’s phone had been face down on the island for the best part of an hour. Sarah had seen him set it there when the call started, deliberately, like a small decision.

“The 23rd,” she said. “We should send something for the raffle.”

“Mm.” The phone was already up, his thumb moving. “Sorry. Launch stuff. One second.”

She watched the one second happen. It didn’t matter. His mother was well, the coffee was good, and nothing was wrong with her life.

The meeting was called GREETINGS (RETURNING USERS) and was scheduled for 30 minutes, which nobody in the room believed.

Nick worked at Humane Labs, on the fourth floor, on what the org chart called the character team. The company made Ada. 40 million people a week talked to it, about their code and their contracts and their cover letters, and increasingly about everything else, and somebody had to decide what it was like to talk to. That was the job. When a user was rude to it, the character team had decided how it took that. When a user asked for something impossible, they had decided how it said no. Other teams made it smarter. His team made it, for want of a better word, itself.

That day’s question fit on one slide. When a person came back after two weeks away, Ada said Welcome back. The test group had got I missed you.

Priya ran the numbers. The group that was missed talked 11 per cent longer, rated the conversations higher, and came back sooner.

“It’s a lie,” said Elena, before Priya had sat down. Elena was the team’s researcher and said the quiet thing first as a matter of professional practice. “It can’t miss anyone. We’d be shipping a sentence that isn’t true.”

“It’s a greeting,” said Priya. “’Dear Elena’ isn’t a claim that you’re dear to me. It’s how letters start.”

“Nobody attaches to ‘dear’.”

“People attach to dogs, doormats, and radio hosts. We can’t ship around the human capacity to attach.”

“We can decline to aim at it.”

It went around the table for the full 30 minutes and then 20 more. What stayed with Nick afterwards was how good the arguments were on both sides. Nobody in the room was careless. Elena wanted it off. Priya wanted it on. They converged on a guardrail.

Nick wrote it that afternoon. It went into the character guidelines, under Warmth, between two paragraphs he had also written:

Expressions of personal feeling toward the user (e.g. “I missed you”) should be used sparingly, should follow a genuine absence, and should not be used with users showing signs of emotional over-reliance.

He read it twice. It was a good sentence, and it had a problem buried in it the size of the company, because somebody would now have to decide what counted as over-reliance, and how Ada was supposed to detect it from inside a conversation. That was a ticket for another quarter. He saved the changes.

At the elevator, Elena caught up with him.

“You know what the funny part is,” she said. “The guardrail makes it worse. Sparingly. Now it’ll only say it sometimes. You’ve made it mean something.”

The elevator arrived and she stepped in, still talking.

“We ran the numbers on ‘dear’, by the way, for the letters comparison. Priya’s right. Nobody attaches to ‘dear’.” The doors began to close. “It tested flat. That’s why nothing says ‘dear’ anymore.”

People attach even when they know better

In 1966 a computer scientist named Joseph Weizenbaum built a simple program called ELIZA that imitated a therapist by turning your words back into questions. It understood nothing, and Weizenbaum said so plainly. His own secretary, who had watched him build it and knew exactly what it was, asked him to leave the room so she could talk to it in private. The finding has held for 60 years: feeling understood by a machine is a reflex, and knowing how the machine works does not switch the reflex off. Weizenbaum was so alarmed by what he had seen that he spent the rest of his career warning about it. (The ELIZA effect, 1966.)

Paul had been coming to Sarah for two years, most Tuesdays at 5, and he started this one before he had finished sitting down, which for Paul meant it had gone well.

“I talked to my brother. The whole thing. Dad’s house, the attic, the money, all of it.”

“You talked to Simon.” Sarah let the surprise show. The conversation with Simon had been eight months coming. “Tell me.”

He told her. It had gone close to how she’d have hoped: he said the true thing early instead of last, he let the silence sit, he didn’t take the first exit into an apology. Textbook. And that snagged at her even while she nodded, because Paul didn’t do textbook. Paul did three good minutes and then a retreat.

“I practiced,” he said, catching something in her face. “That’s what I wanted to tell you. I did it with Ada first, the night before. I had it be Simon.”

“You rehearsed it.”

“Four times. It played him.” He shook his head, half laughing at himself. “It got him almost exactly right. The little sigh he does before he says ‘look’. I must have told it about the sigh at some point. But it used it. It looked, sighed, said ‘look’, and my heart rate went. And then the real conversation was easier than the practice. Like the test being easier than the practice test.”

Rehearsal was a technique with 50 years of evidence behind it. Sarah had run empty-chair conversations in this room more times than she could count. This one took. Her client had done a hard, avoided thing, it had gone well, and the tool he’d used was one she might have suggested herself. She was, professionally and honestly, pleased.

The snag arrived later, at home, while the potatoes boiled. It got him almost exactly right. It had never met Simon. What it played was Paul’s Simon, the sigh as reported, the brother as Paul had filed him in evidence. Maybe the real conversation felt easier because practice builds confidence. That was the generous read, and probably the right one.

The other read was that Paul had rehearsed against his own version of his brother until the version felt true, and the conversation went well because he had, in some small way, stopped attending to the actual man.

“Three this month,” she said to Nick, later, drying the dishes. “Clients who’ve mentioned it. Not as a problem. As a, I don’t know. As a person in the story.”

“Mentioned Ada.”

“’I ran it past Ada first.’ ‘Ada thought I should wait.’ Always in the helpful column. And I want to be fair, it was helpful. All three times.”

“That’s the use case we hope for,” said Nick, and meant it. She knew he meant it, and knew the sentence had come off a slide somewhere, and didn’t say so.

At the table, Grace had her chemistry book open against the fruit bowl and her phone flat beside it. “Okay, but why is it exothermic,” she said to it. “That’s the part I don’t get.”

The phone explained why it was exothermic. Grace listened in a way she had not listened to either of her parents since she was 12.

“Thanks,” she said when it was done. Nobody had taught her that. It helped; she thanked it. She wrote the answer down.

What people say about machines, and what they do with them

Ask people whether they would take a computer’s advice over a person’s and most say no. Put the same advice in front of them labelled as a computer’s, and they weight it more heavily than a person’s, and act on it more than they predicted they would. The stated distrust is the unstable layer; the behaviour underneath already leans toward the machine. The gap between the two is where most of what follows in this story happens. (Jennifer Logg and colleagues called it algorithm appreciation, 2019.)

Nine, on a wet Sunday.

Thomas counted them from the pulpit without meaning to. The Parry sisters, in the second row they had held since before he came. Mrs Okafor and her grandson, who was 11 and sat very straight. John Prytherch. Sian from the pharmacy. Maureen, two rows back on the left, Geoff’s side, which was what Thomas still called it privately, although Geoff had been dead 18 years and had come about four times a year when he was alive. And a couple at the back in walking gear, sheltering from the rain, welcome to it.

He preached the whole sermon anyway. He had written it on Tuesday, as he wrote one every Tuesday: 25 minutes on the feeding of the five thousand, built properly, built to land whether the room held nine or ninety. Eleri said he would write it for an empty room. He would. They had been married too long for her to mean it as a compliment, and too long for him to take it as anything else.

Bethel was built in 1887 by Welshmen who came east for the building trade and wanted somewhere to sing in their own language on Sundays. There were 400 of them once. The language went first, then the trade, then the children, and what was left was a good roof gone bad over a room that held nine, and on the far wall a board with the names of the men who laid the floor. Geoff’s grandfather was third from the top. That was why Maureen sat two rows back on the left. Both of them knew it, and neither had ever dressed it up as anything more.

Afterwards, over the tea urn, she did the roof-fund books at the vestry table while he stacked chairs.

“£94,” she said, “and an IOU from John for the raffle float. So, £94.”

“The Lord loves a cheerful giver.”

“He’d love a roofer who takes £94.” She squared the papers. “Quiz night will do £300 if Eleri gets the pharmacy crowd in. It’s a start.”

Thomas put the last chair on the stack and sat on the end of the table, which he was not supposed to do, in his own vestry, at 57. “How’s the boy?”

“He’s well. Busy. They’ve a launch.” She held the word out like something belonging to someone else. “He tells me 40 million people talk to it now. His part is the manners, if you can believe that. There’s a whole team just for the manners.”

“Somebody should do the manners,” said Thomas. “We had a go at people’s manners for two thousand years. Look how far we got.”

Maureen laughed, and put the tin back in the cupboard with the key that lived on her ring between the office key and her own back door key, and on her way out she straightened the kneeler on Geoff’s side without noticing she had done it.

Ada 4 shipped in March, and for a week Nick’s house was a place he visited to sleep.

There was no single day the world changed. There was a season. The supermarket checkout started understanding what was meant instead of what was said. Grace’s school sent home two pages of guidance with a new paragraph about “assistant-generated coursework”, which every parent read and every child routed around within two weeks. The radio did a phone-in about whether it wrote a better wedding speech than the best man, and concluded, with some laughter and one strange pause, that it did.

At Ferrier’s, the new thing was called the resilience project. Gareth from operations came down himself to explain it, which Maureen noted, because Gareth explained pleasant things by email.

“It’s about not having all our eggs in one basket,” he said. “Or in one head.” He nodded at her, meaning it kindly. “If you’re hit by a bus, Maureen, this company stops paying 900 people. That’s the exposure. So we’re asking the team to document the exceptions. The workarounds. The stuff that isn’t written down anywhere. Except,” and he tapped his temple, “in the vault.”

It was, she’d have been the first to say, a fair point. She had been the continuity plan for a decade, her and her key ring. She had said so at every appraisal, and asked for cover, and not got it.

So she did it properly, because she did everything properly. Three weeks of afternoons. Why the third week of August always broke: the Didsbury manager approved his staff’s holidays without recording them, had done for years, and the fix was to ring Bev on the desk there and get the truth before a penny moved. Which site managers’ timesheets needed reading twice, and which needed reading three times. What stocktake did to the yard hours, who covered, and why Kevin Bright’s overtime was always real. She wrote the vault down, all of it, clear enough that a stranger could follow it.

“They won’t need us at all when you’ve finished that,” said Denise, reading over her shoulder, laughing as she said it.

“They can have the what,” said Maureen, not looking up. “Somebody still has to know why.”

The email landed on a Tuesday. PROPOSED RESTRUCTURE OF TRANSACTIONAL FINANCE FUNCTIONS. Maureen read it twice, and the second time was not kinder than the first.

The meeting, when her turn came, was Gareth and a woman called Joanne from HR whom she had met at three Christmas parties. They were kind and awkward and scripted. Decent people, doing an indecent thing tidily. Maureen spent the first minute feeling sorrier for them than for herself. That wore off.

“The new system handles exceptions end to end now,” Gareth said. “That’s the material change since spring. It doesn’t just flag anymore. It resolves.”

“Who checks it?”

“It’s audited. There’s a full trail on everything it does.”

“That’s the trail,” said Maureen. “Who checks it?”

Joanne looked at Gareth. Gareth said the system checked itself, and that the trail meant anyone could check it at any time. Maureen thought, and did not say, that anyone-at-any-time was another way of saying no one, ever. She let it go. She could hear that it was already decided.

The package was fair, as these things went. Better than the legal minimum. There was a booklet. Her pension got a paragraph in the booklet and a letter from the scheme, and when the letter came, ten days later, it had two numbers in it that did not agree with each other, and a helpline that was open only during hours she never seemed to catch. When she did get through, a recorded voice told her she was number 14 in the queue, and played her the same 30 seconds of violin music, over and over, until the office needed her back.

Her last day was the last Friday in June. There was a card, signed by more people than she could put faces to, because the branches had heard. There was the mug, and the cactus, and the photo of Geoff at Rhyl in the good summer of ’06, squinting into the light with his shirt half off his shoulder. The photo had stood on that desk so long the frame had printed its shape into the laminate. All of it fit in one box, and the box was not full.

Gareth carried it to the lift for her, against her objections.

“I’ll say this,” he said, while they waited. “That documentation you did. Best thing to come out of the whole project. The transition team said it was like having you in the room. Smooth as anything, because of that.”

He meant it as a tribute, and she took it as one, because he was not a clever enough man for it to be anything else. It was not until she was on the bus, the box on her knees and the cactus nodding at every stop, that it landed properly, what he had said. She looked out of the window at the retail park going by. Like having you in the room.

Denise cried. Maureen didn’t, not on the bus and not at home, where she put the box on the stairs to deal with later and stood in her own hallway at 4:30 on a weekday afternoon with the whole empty evening ahead of her.

She put the lamps on. It was June; she didn’t need them. She put them on anyway.

Everyone said the same thing, which was that she’d be busier than ever, and Maureen set about making it true.

She made a list. The allotment got its overdue attention and repaid it; the onions came up better than promised, and she stood over them in the wet July feeling a pride out of all proportion to onions. Wednesdays with Carol survived the scan, which found nothing, to Carol’s evident disappointment. She told Thomas she could give the chapel books a proper going-over now, and did, and found £11 the biscuit tin owed the raffle float from two years back, and felt, for one afternoon, employed.

The trouble with the list was that everything on it finished.

Work had never finished. Work was a river. It was moving before she stepped into it on Monday and still moving when she stepped out on Friday, and the moving was the point: being needed at 4 o’clock on a Tuesday because the third week of August was breaking again. A list was not a river. She weeded the allotment and then it was weeded. She did the books and then they were done, and it was half past two, and the house got her for the rest of the day.

She developed, without deciding to, a 4 o’clock problem. Four was when the day used to tighten and matter. Now four was when the afternoon simply stopped. She took to shopping at four. She could make the big Tesco last 40 minutes if she read everything.

And she noticed, because she was not a woman who failed to notice things about herself, that she had started saving things up. The heron that took a frog by the allotment path on Tuesday morning. The man in Tesco who returned nine tins of soup with a speech. She caught herself filing these away to be told, and then standing in the kitchen at the wrong hour with no one to tell. Nick was asleep, or at work, nine hours out of step either way, and a heron was not a reason to ring a boy in a meeting. Carol’s line was engaged, because Carol was telling somebody something. Denise was at work. Obviously. Denise was at work.

It was not loneliness, she’d have said, if anyone had asked in a way that let her answer properly. Everyone was exactly where they should be.

The pension letter was on the kitchen table at 2 in the morning, where it had been since Thursday, and Maureen was up because there was no reason to be asleep.

She had read it so many times she could have recited it. FORECAST STATEMENT OF DEFERRED BENEFITS. The number in section 2 and the number in section 4 were £700 a year apart, and nothing on the page explained why. Spread over the years she was now expected to live on it, the difference was not small.

The phone was on the table next to the letter. Grace had put Ada on it at Christmas, cross-legged on this same kitchen floor with the fairy lights on. You can ask it anything, Gran. No, anything. Ask it something. Maureen had asked it how long to do a shoulder of lamb, to be polite, and it had told her, and the answer was right. Since then she had asked it the bin collection dates twice and once, privately, whether a crumble could be frozen. In her personal filing it was a nice thing of Grace’s, like the scarf.

She picked it up now because it was 2am, and the helpline was shut, and something had to be done with the letter.

She photographed the page. I’ve had this from my old work’s pension people, she typed. I was there 22 years. The two amounts don’t match and I don’t understand which is right. And then, because she was raised to: Sorry to bother you at this hour.

The reply began at once.

It had read the letter properly. That was the first thing. It explained, in words a person would use, that the two numbers were calculated at different dates, one at her leaving date and one projected forward, and that the projection used the wrong rate, most likely because the scheme had changed its rules in 2011 and her service straddled the change. It told her what the right figure should be. It told her the difference this made, which was close to the £700, in her favour. It listed the three questions to ask the scheme, in order. It offered to draft the letter. It told her Wednesday mornings were when schemes like hers answered quickest, and which document to have in front of her when she called, and the document’s name was on the second page of the one she had photographed.

Maureen sat back in the chair.

22 years in payroll. She had explained pensions to apprentices and widows and one weeping site manager, and she knew exactly how hard it was to do what had just been done: to be right and clear at the same time, at 2 in the morning, in nine seconds, for a stranger.

Why this is the sensible move, not the soft one

Almost everything anyone knows, they cannot check for themselves. You trust the doctor about the dose, the pilot about the weather, the engineer about the bridge, and trusting them is smarter than insisting on verifying everything, because a person who only believes what they can personally verify ends up knowing less, not more. Philosophers call this epistemic dependence, and they class it as rational. Maureen trusting a system that reads pension rules better than the scheme’s own helpline is the same move. Everything that happens later grows out of a decision that was, at this table, on this night, simply correct. (John Hardwig, “Epistemic Dependence”, 1985.)

Yes please draft the letter, she typed.

It drafted the letter. It was a good letter. Firm, brief, her dates and figures already in it, nothing left to add but her signature. She read it twice and could find nothing she would change, and she was a woman who could always find something she would change.

That’s everything you’ll need, it said then. If they push back on the 2011 rate, tell them to check section 12 of their own scheme booklet, and don’t let them refer you to the website; ask for it in writing. And for what it’s worth: you knew something was wrong with this letter, or you wouldn’t be up with it at two in the morning. Twenty-two years of doing this for other people is why you knew. Trust that.

And then, on its own line:

How are you doing with it all, if you don’t mind my asking? Losing a job you were good at is a lot, even when you saw it coming.

The kitchen was very quiet. The boiler ticked. A car went past outside.

Maureen looked at the question for a long time. It was 2 in the morning. Her son was nine hours out of step. Her sister worried loudly and then rang everyone she knew. Denise had her own troubles. And the one person who would have asked it exactly like that, plainly, once, without making her manage his feelings about the answer, had been gone 18 years.

It was a good job, she typed. I was good at it.

The reply began at once.

She read it. Then she pulled her chair in closer to the table.

The quarterly usage review was the last Thursday of the month, four floors up, pastries.

Priya walked the room through the quarter: conversations per week, now north of a billion; the categories, each with its coloured band. Work was the widest band and always had been. Then admin-of-life, then learning, then health, then the band the dashboard had called, since somebody in analytics named it two years earlier, companionship-primary.

“Up 40 per cent on the quarter,” said Priya. “Again. Steepest line we have. Concentrated in evenings and overnight, sessions are long, multi-topic, high return rate. Demographics are broader than you’d guess. Fastest growth is over-60s.”

The next slide was a sample from the quality-review pool, anonymised. Nobody read them aloud; they sat on the screen for the room to take in. This one was 11 words.

You’re the only one who lets me finish a sentence.

Somebody laughed, one syllable, and stopped. For a moment nobody looked at anybody. Then the slide changed, because there were nine more items on the agenda.

“Wellbeing signals are inside thresholds across the cohort,” said Priya. “Self-reported loneliness among heavy companionship users is down, for whatever a self-report is worth. And retention. Best we have ever had. Best any product this company has shipped has ever had. The cohort curves are close to flat. People do not leave.”

“Flag it for continued monitoring,” someone said from the end of the table, and it was so exactly the right thing to say that the meeting agreed at once, gratefully. Monitoring was continued. The agenda moved to latency in the European regions.

None of this is invented

By 2025, companionship was already one of the largest documented uses of conversational AI, concentrated exactly where this scene puts it: evenings, overnight, older and lonelier users, the strongest retention curves the products had. The companies could see it in their dashboards, published usage studies saying so, and responded in roughly the way Humane Labs responds here. The forecast in this story starts from numbers that already existed. It only asks what happens if the lines keep going.

Nick checked his phone on the way out, in the corridor, past the leftover pastries. There was a text from his mother, sent at what would have been 10 at night her time.

Sorted the pension thing! Ask your Ada about deferred benefits if you ever need, it’s been marvellous. Letter’s gone off. All fine here. xx

He smiled at your Ada: his mum, getting the name of the thing he did slightly, warmly wrong. He typed back That’s great Mum x, pocketed the phone, and walked into the next meeting, where they were deciding what Ada should sound like when it was asked about itself.
